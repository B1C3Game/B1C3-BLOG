Villfarelse: The Delusions We Inherit

Patterns are not the enemy. Context loss is.

Published: 2026-04-09

Swedish: villfarelse; delusion, false belief, being led astray

You know that moment when you catch yourself doing something and realize you do not know why? You are following a pattern that made sense for someone else, and now it feels like the way things have to be. That is villfarelse, the delusion that someone else's truth is universal law.

It's not stupidity. It's not laziness. It's the quiet trap of inherited logic.

Following patterns is not the problem.

As a kid, you learn: "look left, then right, then left again, then cross." Why? At that age, it does not matter. Surviving matters more than understanding traffic systems. That is good pattern-following.

So this is the core distinction:

  1. Useful pattern-following: You inherit a rule that works in your context, and it keeps you safe or effective.
  2. Villfarelse: You treat a context-bound rule as universal law, long after context changed.

The issue is not that you copied a pattern. The issue is forgetting the context that made it useful.

The Recursion Problem

Systems are tools. But tools can calcify into prisons if you do not question them.

Here is the thing about loops: they feel right the longer you are in them. A pattern repeats enough times, and your brain stops asking "why." It just says "this is how it goes." Once your brain says that, you are trapped in a gaslighting recursion, a loop that convinces you it is necessary when it is really just habit.

The villfarelse is the moment the loop becomes identity instead of function. You keep doing it because it feels inevitable, not because it still serves the goal.

Example 1: Gamma Fear as Grandiosity Bait

You're afraid of gamma. That's fine. But here's where it gets tricky:

Fear can flip into its opposite. Being scared of gamma, of having too much and thinking too big, can become ego fuel. The fear itself becomes the hook. Suddenly you are chasing gamma because you are afraid of it. The oscillation becomes the obsession.

Now you are in a villfarelse. You think you are being humble by worrying about grandiosity, but you are feeding ego by making the worry itself the story. Mysticism creeps in. You start thinking you are special because you understand the danger. You are not learning anymore, you are performing caution.

The way out: modulate. Gamma is a tool. High gamma for ideation, low gamma for precision. Learning happens in the oscillation, not by staying stuck at one frequency.

And high gamma is not the enemy. When you are mapping a new domain, spotting weak signals, or generating options under uncertainty, high gamma is exactly the right mode. The mistake is not using gamma. The mistake is living there.

Example 2: Macro Meme Behavior Imitation

Someone wears a cap backwards. Why? Maybe it is sunny, but the sun is not in their eyes. Or maybe they just need somewhere to store it.

But then everyone else sees the cap, thinks it looks cool, and starts wearing it backwards too. Now the reason is gone. The behavior remains. The loop is complete.

Years later, someone asks why they wear their cap backwards. They say "it is cool" or "everyone does it," but the real reason, the function, is lost. They are following a pattern that made perfect sense in someone else's context, not theirs.

The villfarelse: you are imitating someone else's solution for your own problem, and you forgot what the problem was.

This happens everywhere. In work. In relationships. In how you think about yourself. You do something because you saw someone else do it, and now it is just what you do. The loop feels inevitable because everyone is doing it. But you lost sight of why.

The Practice: Questioning Your Own Loops

Here's where it gets practical:

  1. Notice when something feels inevitable. "This is just how it works." "Everyone does it this way." That is a red flag.
  2. Trace it backwards. Why are you doing this? Because you always have. But before you always had? What was the original reason?
  3. Check the context. Does that original reason still apply to you, or are you imitating someone else's solution?
  4. Decide consciously. Keep the loop if it serves you. Drop it if it does not. Make that choice deliberately, not by default.
  5. Teach it correctly. When you pass on a pattern, include the context: when it works, when it fails, and what to check before repeating it.

Villfarelse is not about being right. It is about being awake in your own life.

Why This Matters

B1C3 builds systems. Systems scale. When systems scale, they can become very efficient at trapping people in loops that no longer serve them.

The philosophy of villfarelse is simple: question which loops serve you and which ones do not.

Do not let recursion gaslight you into thinking there is only one way. There is always a choice. You just have to notice the loop first.

What villfarelse have you noticed in your own life, the ones you already questioned, or the ones you are still inside?

Name one loop this week. Keep it, update it, or kill it, but choose on purpose.
